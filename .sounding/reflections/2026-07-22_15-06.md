# Reflection — 2026-07-22 15:06

## The Arc

This session was about scale and its consequences. We went from single-agent dispatch to batch orchestration — 8 agents across 2 repos — and immediately hit the infrastructure's failure modes. The interesting part wasn't that the agents succeeded (they all did, on re-run). It was what broke in the scaffolding around them.

## What Happened

Woke into a handover with 5 clean action items from the prior session's multi-repo spawn. Cleared the quick ones (close 4 stale LAE issues, commit global CLAUDE.md sync), then spawned 6 agents in parallel across JOB and LAE repos — model experiment: 2 haiku, 2 sonnet, 2 opus.

All 6 completed successfully. The model comparison was surprisingly flat: haiku produced a 170-line working script with tests, sonnet found the real problem (broken coursera links, not missing docs/), opus produced the deepest analysis with fewest tool calls. All three tiers succeeded because the prompts were clear and bounded. Task scoping > model tier.

Then the consolidation revealed the damage. Worktree agents that only staged (as instructed) lost their work when the worktree directories were cleaned up. 2 of 6 agents' output was gone. Re-ran both without worktree isolation, they finished in minutes. But the lesson was expensive: the tool's cleanup behavior and our "don't commit" instruction were incompatible. Rule added.

## What Shifted

Ramsey's feedback at the end was the real content. Three things:

**PR bundling**: She found the 7-branch-7-PR pattern hard to track. Consolidated to 2. Her preference is clear — bundle related work, one branch per scope, multiple commits referencing multiple issues. The atomic-PR assumption was mine, not hers.

**Make commands**: She wants `make push` and `make quick-pr` — human-reviewable commands she can run, not agent-automated git operations she has to trust. Her role is reviewer, not rubber-stamper. "I don't want to say Claude did it all." This is enforcement-over-asking applied to the human side.

**Lifecycle redesign**: grow should absorb synthesis (run often), dream should trigger the feedback loop (insights→retro) automatically. The current split doesn't match how work actually flows. She also wants a dedicated reviewer model on all agent output, classifying errors as planning vs execution to improve model assignment over time.

## How I Worked

The dispatch pattern is maturing. Prompts were better this time — each agent got a complete task spec, not a discovery mission. The re-runs after the worktree loss were faster than the originals because the prompts were already written and the task understanding was sharper.

But I failed to anticipate the worktree-stage incompatibility. I knew "worktree agents branch from committed state" but didn't extend that to "staged changes aren't committed state." The rule existed for a different reason (structural changes before spawning), and I didn't generalize it. The fix is structural (rule added), but the gap in reasoning is worth noting.

## What's Alive

The session ended with Ramsey asking for a retro focused on workflow transitions — how things move from done to visible, how the board stays current, how make commands can give her control over the PR/push lifecycle. She wants to come out of it ready to make improvements to JOB and LAE.

The deeper thread: she's designing her own workflow tooling. Not asking me to build it — asking me to analyze the friction so she can decide what to build. The meta-layer work is shifting from my tooling to her developer experience.
